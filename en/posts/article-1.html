From Cybercafé Clerk to Tech Giant: Riding Three Tech Waves, I Choose to Clock Out on Time in the AI Era

I don't possess a glamorous academic pedigree. My highest initial qualification is an associate degree, and my very first job was a cybercafé clerk. Back then, I spent my off-hours teaching myself web design and Java programming. To be completely honest, compared to my peers graduating from elite universities, I was profoundly aware of the gap in our foundations.

But fortune favored me. My first formal development role landed me at a Japanese outsourcing firm. It was an era before the internet industry had truly taken off. Under the high-intensity, rigorous standards of Japanese corporate culture, I hammered out an exceptionally precise system of technical logic.

Many have asked me: How does someone starting from such a humble baseline manage to thrive within major tech giants for so many years? Looking back, an elite degree merely grants you an entrance ticket. It was that "uncompromisingly meticulous" logic cultivated during my early days that became my defensive moat, enabling me to stand firm in complex engineering systems later on.

1. The Three Technical Waves I Rode

Reviewing the past 20 years feels like sitting on a rollercoaster bridging tech giants and entrepreneurial ventures. I happened to catch several critical moments and executed the right moves at the right time:

  • The First Wave: The Transition Era of IE6 Preservation. It was a time of obsessively debugging IE6 compatibility. Today's younger generation can scarcely imagine how deep we had to dig into the box model or the infamous Double Margin Bug. That was my first technical dividend; relying on an absolute, heavy mastery of low-level frontend behavior, I secured my entry ticket to JD.com.
  • The Second Wave: The Industrial Revolution of Browsers. This was the golden age of the Vue framework and Chrome's rapid iterations. Running my own startup at the time gave me sharp foresight; while the majority adopted a wait-and-see approach, I led my team to pioneer Vue adoption early. Moving from raw DOM manipulation to data-driven interfaces yielded the most lucrative dividends of frontend engineering.
  • The Third Wave: The Leadership Dividends Crafted through Entrepreneurship. The trials and tribulations of running a business forged hard-earned skills in project management and business insight. Consequently, when I later joined Beike, I wasn't just a coder—I was someone who utilized Scrum thinking and commercial frameworks to break down highly complex system architectures.

2. AI Arrived, but Deep Down, I Am Content to Stay Put

The dawn of the AI era is far more disruptive than any previous technological shift. It is not a mere tool upgrade; it is a fundamental restructuring of logic.

I am acutely aware that this represents the fourth wave. In fact, I recognized its potential during its earliest inception and adopted AI application workflows quite early. I instantly felt that this might be the grandest paradigm shift of my lifetime. Yet, I noticed a profound, physiological resistance surfacing from deep within.

This resistance does not stem from an inability to learn. It comes from being genuinely exhausted by the endless rat race. AI makes me feel as though much of my past meticulous craft has been abstractly rendered obsolete.

Having navigated tech conglomerates for years and weathered countless startup failures, my brain functions like an overloaded hard drive—every single sector packed to capacity with Sprints, Backlogs, and unceasing deployments. When an entity like AI emerges, capable of iterating 24/7 without pause, my immediate instinct wasn't to charge forward. It was simply: let's power down.

"We fought for 20 years not to remain slaves to code in the AI era, but to earn the freedom of not being defined by our work."

3. From "Total Control" to a Sense of Void

Writing code used to offer a form of "artisan joy." Fine-tuning IE6 compatibility felt like a true craft; structuring an elegant Vue component felt like creating a piece of art.

Modern AI-assisted development, however, feels more like a game of probabilistic gambling. You orchestrate models, refine prompts, and assemble pieces. Efficiency has undeniably skyrocketed a thousandfold, but that intimate sensation of manually polishing an industrial masterpiece has vanished. For a veteran accustomed to foundational, absolute logic, this "black-box" production pipeline induces a deep, systemic sense of emptiness.

Since efficiency is no longer accumulated through sheer human hours, shouldn't a member of the legacy workforce like myself gracefully step aside, leaving the stage to energetic youths who can seamlessly match AI's frantic tempo?

4. In Penang: Launching My "Life Sprint"

Today, I live in Penang, Malaysia.

My Backlog has shifted. It is no longer about patching production bugs on Jira; instead, it consists of:

  • Monitoring my two daughters' academic progress at their international school.
  • Savoring a fresh cup of white coffee at 3:00 PM when the sunlight strikes perfectly.
  • Utilizing the logical analytical skills honed over 20 years to dissect the world via my official blog, rather than refactoring enterprise code.

I still monitor AI closely, but I am no longer an active frontline participant. I am content being an observer. I let AI simplify my life and design my travel itineraries, treating it as an assistant rather than a competitor.

5. A Heartfelt Note to Fellow Engineers

If you find yourself hovering around the age of 40 and feel overwhelmed by the ferocious AI tide, let me tell you this: admitting you are tired isn't a defeat—it is a profoundly clear-headed reallocation of your life assets.

We spent two decades striving not to stay chained to a keyboard under new AI masters, but to achieve the ultimate luxury: the freedom to not be defined by a corporate title.

I have enjoyed the peaks of tech waves and survived the troughs of entrepreneurial valleys. Now, I choose to step away from the trenches of primary development with a completely peaceful mind. I am giving life back to myself, and leaving the anxiety behind for the era to sort out.
